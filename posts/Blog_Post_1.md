## From Brief to Brainstorm the start of my community

The first step of my project was about understanding what the assignment and BlaBla Corp brief were actually asking us to design. At first, I was thinking of the task more as building a social media-style website with posts, likes and comments. However, after reading the brief more carefully, I realised the focus was more on designing a specific “community hub” where people with a shared interest could connect. To me, this meant the idea had to be unique, not too broad, and based on something that personally interested me enough that I would want to build a site about it.

This became important during early ideation. A successful community hub needs more than just users posting content. It needs a clear reason for people to return, contribute, and interact with each other. The brief also emphasised that:

> “Curating the right experience is what keeps them coming back.”

![Early notes interpreting the brief](assets/images/Brief_Breakdown.png)

*Figure 1: Early notes or sketch interpreting the brief through ideas of community, information, experience and advertising value.*

From there, I began brainstorming possible communities based on my current interests. Some early ideas included photographers, frequent thrift shoppers, surfers looking for the best surf spots, indie game players, and people looking for hidden food spots around USYD and Newtown. Each idea had strengths, but also limitations. For example, photographers already form strong online communities and naturally share visual content, but it was harder to identify a unique feature that would not simply become another image-sharing platform. A surf spot hub had clear local value, but it risked relying heavily on users being active and sharing their locations. A hidden food spots blog was appealing to me because it was local and specific, but it felt like it could become more of a recommendation site than an active community.

![Brainstorm map of community ideas](assets/images/Brainstorm.png)

*Figure 2: Brainstorm map of possible community hub ideas, including home cooks, photographers, thrift shoppers, surf spots, indie games and hidden food spots.*

The idea that stood out most was a community for home cooks. This was an idea that was discussed briefly in class, as Sanjana, my group member, mentioned she had an interest in this area. Home cooking has a strong mixture of practical information and personal experience. People do not only look for recipes; they ask questions, share failures, modify instructions, recommend substitutions, and seek feedback on what they made.

![Comparison table of community ideas](assets/images/Comparison_Table.png)

*Figure 3: Comparison table of community ideas, considering each community’s strengths, weaknesses and prototype potential.*

Overall, the first week helped narrow the project from a broad design brief into a more focused community direction. Home cooks emerged as the strongest option because the community combines repeat participation, user-generated information, practical problem-solving and social feedback. This gave the project a promising foundation for the next stage: discussing ideas as a group, conducting online ethnography, and deciding what would make our home cooking hub distinct from existing recipe platforms.
